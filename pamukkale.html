ABOUT THE DESTINATION

Discover Pamukkale — Turkey's Cotton Castle

Pamukkale, meaning 'Cotton Castle' in Turkish, is one of Turkey's most extraordinary natural wonders. Over thousands of years, mineral-rich thermal water has flowed down the hillside, leaving behind dazzling white travertine terraces filled with pools of warm turquoise water — a landscape unlike anywhere else on Earth.

Crowning the terraces lies Hierapolis, a remarkably preserved ancient spa city founded in the 2nd century BC. Its grand theatre, colonnaded streets, temples and vast necropolis draw visitors from around the world, who come to walk among ruins where people once sought the healing powers of the thermal springs.

At the heart of the site is Cleopatra's Antique Pool, where you can swim among submerged marble columns in naturally warm, mineral-rich water — a truly unforgettable experience. Together, the travertines, Hierapolis and the thermal waters form a UNESCO World Heritage Site of breathtaking beauty.

Our carefully organised full-day tour takes you from Alanya and Side through scenic mountain landscapes to this natural marvel. Whether you're strolling across the white terraces, exploring ancient ruins, or relaxing by the thermal waters, this journey combines nature, history and relaxation in one unforgettable day.

A Brief History of Pamukkale & Hierapolis

The travertines of Pamukkale were formed over hundreds of thousands of years, as calcium-rich thermal water rose from the earth, cooled and deposited soft white calcium carbonate that slowly hardened into the dazzling terraces we see today. People have been drawn to these warm mineral springs since antiquity, long believing in their healing powers.

Around 190 BC the Hellenistic kings of Pergamon founded the spa city of Hierapolis on the plateau above the terraces. Under Roman rule it flourished as a celebrated health resort, with grand thermal baths, a great theatre, temples and one of the largest ancient necropolises in Anatolia. A powerful earthquake in 60 AD destroyed much of the city, which was then rebuilt in the elegant Roman style whose ruins still stand today.

In the Byzantine era Hierapolis became an important Christian centre, linked to the Apostle Philip, who is believed to have been martyred here. After repeated earthquakes the city was gradually abandoned by the 14th century and slowly reclaimed by the white travertines. In 1988 Pamukkale and Hierapolis were together inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List, honoured for both their extraordinary natural beauty and their cultural importance.

The White Travertine Terraces

Cascading snow-white terraces formed over millennia by mineral-rich thermal water. You'll walk barefoot across the warm, shallow pools — the most iconic sight of Pamukkale and the reason it's called the 'Cotton Castle'.

Hierapolis Ancient City

The remarkably preserved ruins of a Greco-Roman spa city that crowns the terraces, with a grand theatre, a colonnaded main street, temples and one of the largest ancient necropolises in Anatolia.

Cleopatra's Antique Pool

A natural thermal pool scattered with ancient marble columns toppled by an earthquake. You can swim here in warm, mineral-rich water among genuine Roman ruins — an optional experience (€15 on-site).
